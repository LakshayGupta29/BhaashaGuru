Quadratic Equations - NCERT Mathematics Class 10

Chapter 4: Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation is a polynomial equation of the second degree. The general form of a quadratic equation is ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants and a ≠ 0.

1. Standard Form
The standard form of a quadratic equation is ax² + bx + c = 0, where:
- a is the coefficient of x² (called the quadratic coefficient)
- b is the coefficient of x (called the linear coefficient)
- c is the constant term

2. Solutions Using Quadratic Formula
The quadratic formula is used to find the roots of a quadratic equation:
x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a

The discriminant Δ = b² - 4ac determines the nature of roots:
- If Δ > 0: Two distinct real roots
- If Δ = 0: Two equal real roots (one repeated root)
- If Δ < 0: No real roots (two complex roots)

3. Methods to Solve Quadratic Equations
a) Factorization Method: Express the quadratic as a product of two linear factors
b) Completing the Square: Convert the equation to a perfect square
c) Using the Quadratic Formula: Apply the formula directly
d) Graphical Method: Find the x-intercepts of the parabola

4. Vieta's Formulas
For a quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0 with roots α and β:
- Sum of roots: α + β = -b/a
- Product of roots: αβ = c/a

5. Applications
Quadratic equations are used in physics, engineering, economics, and many real-world scenarios including projectile motion, optimization problems, and area calculations.

Pythagorean Theorem - NCERT Mathematics

The Pythagorean theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides.

If a triangle has sides a, b, and hypotenuse c, then:
c² = a² + b²

This relationship is fundamental in geometry and trigonometry. The theorem can be proved using various methods including geometric proofs and algebraic manipulation.

Chemical Equations and Balancing - NCERT Chemistry Class 10

Balancing chemical equations is essential to ensure the law of conservation of mass is satisfied. The steps to balance a chemical equation are:

1. Count the atoms of each element on both sides
2. Identify elements that are not balanced
3. Assign coefficients to balance one element at a time
4. Verify that all elements are balanced
5. Check that the equation is in its simplest form

Key principles:
- Coefficients must be whole numbers
- Never change the chemical formulas of compounds
- Start with the most complex compound
- Balance metals, then non-metals, then hydrogen, then oxygen
